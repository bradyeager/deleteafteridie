Chapter One

What Your Family Is About to Find

When someone dies, their family doesn't get a notification that says "here's what to look at." They get access. To everything. Because the phone is there. Because the laptop is there. Because the passwords get shared in a crisis. Because someone needs to cancel the subscriptions.

Of those 130+ accounts, almost none of them have any instructions for what happens when you die. No designated recipient. No deletion request. No "if I'm gone, please close this." Just an account sitting there, logged in, full of data, waiting for whoever finds it first.

Here's what they're going to find:

  • Your complete browser history. Every search. Every site. Everything you've looked up at 2am that you'd never want another human being to see.
  • Your saved passwords. Every account you've ever logged into from that browser. All of them. One list.
  • Your Gmail. Every email you've ever sent or received. The ones from your doctor. The ones from your lawyer. The ones from the person you shouldn't have been emailing.
  • Your Google search history. YouTube history. Location history. A documented timeline of everywhere you've been and everything you've searched for — for years.
  • Your dating app profiles. Still active. Still showing up in search results. Still with photos. Still with messages.
  • Your Instagram DMs. The full conversation history. Every message you thought was private.
  • Your local files. The ones in the folders you named something boring so nobody would open them.
  • Your photos. Including the ones in the "Recently Deleted" folder that didn't actually get deleted.

The uncomfortable truth: Most people's phones contain enough private information to completely rewrite how their family remembers them. A single conversation. A single search. A single folder. One thing is all it takes.

You have time to prevent this. Right now. The question is whether you'll use it.

Chapter Two

Platform by Platform:
What Survives You

Not all platforms are created equal. Some have tools to help manage your digital legacy. Most don't. Here's exactly what happens on the major platforms when you die — and how exposed you are.

Gmail / Google Account
Inactive Account Manager exists but almost nobody uses it. Default: account stays active indefinitely. Family can request access with a death certificate — but the process takes weeks and isn't guaranteed. Everything is accessible to anyone with your password.
Critical
Browser History & Passwords
Completely unprotected. Anyone with access to your unlocked computer has instant access to your complete browsing history and every saved password. No platform protection. No delay. No process.
Critical
Dating Apps (Tinder, Bumble, OkCupid)
Profile stays fully active after death. Will continue appearing in other users' feeds. No death verification process. No automatic deletion. The profile of someone who died last week is functionally identical to the profile of someone who's actively swiping.
Critical
Instagram
Memorialization available but requires submission of proof of death. DMs are not protected — anyone with your login can read every private conversation you've ever had. No automatic deletion process exists.
High
Google Photos
Stays active indefinitely. Can be shared or accessed by anyone with your Google login. Includes location metadata on every photo — showing exactly where you were when each image was taken.
High
Google Drive
All documents, files, and shared content remains accessible. Anything you've ever stored, scanned, or created — contracts, medical records, financial documents, personal writing — stays live.
High
Facebook
Has a Legacy Contact and memorialization process — one of the better platform tools. But private messages are still accessible to anyone with your login. Memorialization doesn't delete private content.
Medium

The pattern is consistent: platforms are built to retain your data, not to protect your privacy after death. The business model depends on your content staying on the platform. They have no financial incentive to delete anything. That's your job — before it's too late to do it yourself.

Chapter Three

The Five Things You Need to Do
Before It's Too Late

This is not a "someday" list. Someday doesn't come for 7,500 Americans every single day. Here's what to do — in order — starting today.

01 — Audit Every Account You Own

Open a private browser tab and go through your autofill. Count. Most people have no idea how many accounts they have until they look. Write down every account that contains something you wouldn't want found. Dating apps. Email. Cloud storage. Messaging apps. Private photos. Start with the ones that terrify you most.

The average person has 130+ accounts. You will find things you forgot existed. Old profiles from years ago. Conversations you thought were long gone. They're not gone. They're waiting.

02 — Set Up Google's Inactive Account Manager (Today)

Go to myaccount.google.com → Data & Privacy → More options → Make a plan for your account. Set the inactivity period to 3 months. Choose what gets deleted versus shared. This is Google's built-in tool and it's free. The problem: it only handles Google. Everything else is still wide open. And it requires you to manually set it up, manually keep your account active, and manually update your preferences as your life changes. It's better than nothing — which is what most people have.

03 — Delete What You Can Delete Right Now

You don't need a dead man's switch to delete things you already want gone. Go through your accounts. Delete old dating profiles. Clear your browser history. Remove photos you don't want surviving you. Unsubscribe from platforms you haven't used in years. Do this today. Every account you close now is one less thing to worry about later.

04 — Tell Someone What to Do With Your Accounts

This is the part nobody does. Write down — somewhere secure — which accounts exist and what you want done with each one. Not the passwords. Just the accounts and the instructions. Your trusted person needs to know: "Delete my Gmail. Close my Tinder. Let my family have my Google Photos. Delete everything else." Without instructions, they'll guess — and they'll get it wrong.

05 — Automate It

Here's the problem with Steps 1–4: they require you to do it right now, keep it updated, trust another person to execute correctly, and hope the platforms cooperate. That's four points of failure. A dead man's switch removes all four. It runs automatically. It doesn't need another person. It doesn't rely on platform goodwill. And it activates precisely when it needs to — when you stop checking in.

This is what Delete After I Die was built for.

Chapter Four

The Specific Threats
Most People Don't Think About

Beyond the obvious — email, photos, browser history — there are categories of digital exposure that almost nobody plans for. These are the ones that cause the most damage.

The Dating App Problem

Dating profiles don't die with you. They stay active. Still showing up. Still appearing in other users' feeds. Someone who knew you might stumble across your profile weeks after your funeral. The conversations are still there. The photos are still there. Everything you said to everyone you ever matched with — still there. For a married person, a person in a relationship, or anyone who had a private life on these platforms, this is catastrophic exposure.

The Search History Problem

Your search history is a complete psychological profile of every fear, curiosity, desire, medical symptom, financial problem, and private thought you've had for years. People search things they'd never say out loud to anyone. The search bar is the most honest thing most people interact with every day — because they believe it's private. It's not private after you die. Anyone with access to your browser or Google account has access to all of it.

The "Deleted" Photos Problem

The trash folder exists. Cloud backups exist. "Recently Deleted" exists. When most people delete a photo, it sits in a recoverable state for 30 days on the device and potentially indefinitely in cloud backups. People believe they've deleted things that are completely recoverable by anyone with access to the device or account.

The Location History Problem

Google knows everywhere you've been. Every restaurant, every address, every trip, every detour. Your location history is a complete documented timeline of your physical life. It shows patterns. It shows visits. It shows the places you went that don't appear anywhere else in your digital life — because you were careful about everything except the GPS running silently in your pocket.

The common thread: none of these require someone to be malicious or snooping. They just require access to a phone or computer — which every grieving family member has when they're trying to handle the practical aftermath of a death. They're not looking for secrets. They're just looking. And they find them anyway.

Chapter Five

What a Dead Man's Switch Actually Does

A dead man's switch is not complicated. The name comes from industrial safety — machines that require an operator to actively hold a switch to keep running. Release the switch, the machine stops. No operator, no operation.

In digital form: you check in regularly to prove you're alive. You stop checking in — for whatever reason — and the switch triggers. What happens next is up to you.

There are two kinds of digital dead man's switches. Most people build the wrong one.

The Message-Sender (Not What You Need)

Services like DeadMan'sSwitch.net and similar tools send pre-written emails to designated recipients when you stop checking in. They're useful for sharing passwords with trusted people, notifying contacts, or sending final messages. What they can't do: delete anything. They send. They don't erase. If your goal is to make sure something survives you, they work. If your goal is to make sure something doesn't survive you, they're useless.

The Deletion Engine (What You Actually Need)

A deletion-based dead man's switch doesn't send anything. It removes things. When triggered, it logs into your accounts and deletes what you've told it to delete — dating profiles, email labels, browser history, local files, Google data, photos. Quietly. Automatically. Without anyone needing to do anything.

This is what Delete After I Die does. When you stop checking in, the desktop app triggers, opens your accounts via automated browser sessions using credentials stored only on your machine, and executes the deletions you configured during setup. Your family never knows what was there.

  • Old social media accounts from platforms you've abandoned
  • Shopping accounts with saved payment info you no longer use
  • Forum accounts or communities with old posts you don't want surfaced
  • Email accounts you no longer check (forward anything important, then delete)

Accounts to Configure for Deletion via Dead Man's Switch

  • Active dating profiles (Tinder, Bumble, OkCupid, and any discreet platforms)
  • Gmail (specific labels, conversations, or full inbox)
  • Google Photos (specific albums or full library)
  • Google Drive (specific folders or full drive)
  • Google Search, YouTube, and location history
  • Browser history, autofill, saved passwords (local machine)
  • Local files and folders you've designated for deletion
  • Instagram (DMs and/or full account)

Accounts to Preserve or Transfer

  • Financial accounts — leave access instructions with your estate executor or attorney
  • Photos you want family to have — share an album, don't just leave it accessible
  • Password manager — use the emergency access feature to designate a trusted contact
  • Any accounts with monetary value (crypto, domain names, digital assets)

Platform Tools to Activate Now

  • Google Inactive Account Manager — set to 3 months, configure deletion or sharing
  • Apple Legacy Contact — designate who gets access to iCloud after death
  • Facebook Legacy Contact — choose memorialization vs deletion
  • Password manager emergency access (1Password, Bitwarden, etc.)

Important: Platform tools cover the accounts those platforms control. They don't cover your dating apps, your browser history, your local files, your discreet platforms, or anything that requires you to log in and manually click "Delete." That's the gap Delete After I Die was built to fill.
